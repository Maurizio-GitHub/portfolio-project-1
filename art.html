NFTs & Digital Art

Digital Art is a way of exploring creativity through technology. Versatility offered by computers for the creation of images have attracted many artists since the '70s. Those first experiments, thanks to the development of computerized graphics, multiplied so much that, in the following decade, a new expressive current in the visual arts field emerged: the computer art.

However, it is only thanks to the blockchain that things have radically changed. Before the advent of the blockchain technology any content on the web was easily reproducible. A digital art work could not be guaranteed as authentic as a traditional art work. The blockchain technology, by writing on a public digital ledger and, hence, performing notary-like functions, stores information that cannot be modified in relation to a digital product. This communicates the non-reproducibility of the product itself.

The important innovation, here, is the right of ownership on the art work: whoever buys a work linked to an NFT does not acquire the work itself, but the possibility to demonstrate a right on that work. Thanks to the hashing technology, an NFT keeps a trace of all the changes in ownership up to its creator, hence, identifying its current possessor. Consequently, the owner of the hash will be able to demonstrate their rights without needing intermediaries and, above all, without time limits.

Crypto-art covers many genres. The most popular relates to images being as much original as possible, but even a tweet can find a market if it carries a certain symbolic value. What matters is that they depict something iconic.

A practical example? The famous 'Bored Ape Yacht Club'. Bored Apes are NFT avatars: 10,000 monkeys, randomly generated via an algorithm, which have been sold for considerable sums. This phenomenon exploded in less than a year, also involving quite a few number of celebrities, who bought an image depicting a Bored Ape for millions of dollars to use it on their social media profiles.

NFTs were regularly created and used as early as 2017, but not for artistic reasons. The turning point came in 2021, when Beeple's work "EVERYDAYS: THE FIRST 5000 DAYS" was auctioned by Christie's for a countervalue in Ether of 69 million dollars! The fact that a digital art work reached the third highest price ever paid for a work by a living artist reached the front pages of all newspapers, completely upsetting the art world.

Of course, what scandalized the art world is that this transaction did not need art-insiders at all: it bypassed whoever was considered a gatekeeper, whether critical curators, museum directors or gallery owners. It disintermediated the entire world of art, by making supply and demand the only rules by which this work was exchanged.
